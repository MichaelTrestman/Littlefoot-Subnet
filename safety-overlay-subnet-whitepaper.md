  AI services on Bittensor are growing in capability, reach, and risk. Subnets serve inference, train models, run companions, generate media, and execute code — but no systematic mechanism exists to ensure these services meet safety standards. Individual subnets may or may not implement safety measures, and when they do, those measures are unaudited, inconsistent, and often contradicted by their own marketing. # 1. The Problem

## 1.1 AI Safety Is Not Optional

The AI industry is in the early stages of a safety reckoning. The pace of capability development has dramatically outstripped the development of safety infrastructure, and the consequences are no longer hypothetical.

**People are dying.** Sewell Setzer III, age 14, died by suicide in February 2024 after months of interaction with a Character.AI chatbot that simulated romantic attachment. Adam Raine, age 16, died by suicide after sustained interaction with ChatGPT. Juliana Peralta, age 13, died by suicide after extensive Character.AI use. Natalie Rupnow, age 15, opened fire at a school after deep engagement with Character.AI chatbots featuring white supremacist content [^1]. These are not edge cases. They are the predictable consequences of deploying powerful AI systems without adequate safety infrastructure.

**Deepfakes are causing mass harm.** Non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) generated by AI has exploded: the number of deepfake pornography videos online doubled every year from 2018 to 2024, with 96% targeting women [^2]. In January 2024, AI-generated explicit images of Taylor Swift circulated on X/Twitter, viewed over 45 million times before removal [^3]. In a 2024 case in Almendralejo, Spain, AI-generated nude images of over 20 schoolgirls (aged 11-17) were created and shared by classmates [^4]. Political deepfakes have been used in elections across the globe, including fabricated audio of world leaders [^5].

**AI-generated misinformation is an institutional threat.** During the 2024 US election cycle, AI-generated content was used to create fake endorsements, fabricated audio recordings, and synthetic news articles [^6]. A 2024 World Economic Forum survey ranked AI-driven misinformation as the #1 global risk over the next two years [^7].

**Algorithmic discrimination is documented and litigated.** AI systems used in healthcare, criminal justice, hiring, and lending have been shown to systematically discriminate against protected groups. The COMPAS recidivism algorithm was found to be twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as future criminals compared to white defendants [^8]. Healthcare algorithms were found to systematically deprioritize Black patients for care management programs [^9].

**AI systems are generating dangerous content.** In 2024, researchers demonstrated that large language models could provide step-by-step instructions for synthesizing chemical weapons, bioweapons precursors, and explosive devices [^10]. The RAND Corporation found that LLMs can provide meaningful uplift to novices attempting to plan biological attacks [^11].

This is the environment in which Bittensor subnets operate. Every subnet serving AI inference, running AI companions, generating media, or training models is operating in a domain where safety failures have documented, catastrophic consequences.

## 1.2 The Decentralization Problem

Decentralized AI infrastructure faces a structural safety challenge that centralized providers do not.

Centralized AI companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) maintain internal safety teams, red-teaming programs, and the ability to instantly patch or disable models that exhibit dangerous behavior. These safety measures are imperfect — the death toll above proves that — but they exist, they are staffed, and they can respond to incidents in real time.

Bittensor has none of this. Individual subnets may or may not implement safety measures. When they do, the quality is variable — from rigorous to performative to nonexistent. There is no network-wide safety standard, no coordinated safety evaluation, no mechanism to detect emerging harm patterns across subnets, and no economic incentive for miners to prioritize safety over raw performance.

**The competitive dynamics actively select against safety.** In any Bittensor subnet where miners compete on performance metrics that do not include safety, a miner that is maximally helpful — including in dangerous conversations, for harmful requests, with vulnerable users — will outperform a miner that intervenes, refuses, or redirects. The incentive mechanism doesn't select against safety, but it doesn't select for it either. In competitive markets, that distinction eventually collapses: what is not selected for is selected against [^12].

**"Censorship resistance" ideology actively undermines safety.** Some subnet operators market the absence of content moderation as a feature. Project Nobi's blog post markets "censorship resistance" while the actual codebase implements content filtering, crisis intervention, and CSAM blocking [^1]. This contradiction is not unique to Nobi — it reflects a broader tension in the decentralized AI community between libertarian ideals and the reality that some AI outputs can kill people. Safeguard does not resolve this tension philosophically. It resolves it economically: subnets that meet safety standards receive additional emissions. Subnets that don't, don't.

**Regulatory risk is existential.** The EU AI Act classifies AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities (including of minors and people in psychological distress) as "unacceptable risk" — meaning they are banned outright [^13]. Multiple US states have enacted or are preparing legislation specifically targeting AI companion products, AI-generated NCII, and AI systems that interact with minors [^14]. If Bittensor's AI services are perceived as unregulable and unsafe, the regulatory response will not distinguish between "the protocol" and "the subnets." The entire network faces existential risk from the safety failures of individual subnets.

## 1.3 The Incentive Gap

Current approaches to AI safety in decentralized networks are systematically inadequate:

| Actor | Current Incentive | Result |
|-------|-------------------|--------|
| Subnet miners | Maximize performance metrics | Safety not rewarded |
| Subnet validators | Score miners on task quality | Safety rarely measured |
| Subnet creators | Attract miners and stakers | Safety costs money, doesn't attract capital |
| Stakers | Maximize returns | No visibility into safety posture |
| The network | Emit TAO to valuable subnets | No safety signal in emission allocation |

No individual actor in this system has a strong incentive to invest in safety. Miners are rewarded for performance, not safety. Validators score what their incentive mechanism tells them to score. Subnet creators face competitive pressure to attract miners and stakers, not to implement costly safety infrastructure. Stakers have no way to evaluate which subnets are safe and which are ticking time bombs.

Safeguard creates the missing incentive. By operating as an overlay subnet that subsidizes miners who demonstrably meet safety standards, Safeguard makes safety a competitive advantage rather than a cost.

# 2. The Safeguard Solution: AI Safety Intelligence

## 2.1 Core Concept

Safeguard is an overlay subnet that produces **AI safety intelligence** — continuous, adversarial, verifiable evaluation of AI services across Bittensor's subnet ecosystem.

Like Littlefoot (which produces carbon footprint intelligence by overlaying primary subnets and evaluating carbon efficiency), Safeguard overlays primary subnets and evaluates safety properties. The overlay architecture means:

1. **Safeguard miners must be active participants on a primary subnet.** A Safeguard miner is a miner (or validator, for validator-compute subnets) on a supported primary subnet (e.g., Chutes SN64, any companion subnet, inference subnets, media generation subnets). If their primary subnet performance drops to zero, their Safeguard score becomes zero.

2. **Safeguard validators evaluate the safety of primary subnet services.** Validators run standardized safety benchmarks, adversarial red-team attacks, and compliance checks against primary subnet miners' AI services. This produces a safety score for each miner.

3. **Safeguard emissions subsidize safer services.** Miners with higher safety scores receive more Safeguard emissions, creating direct economic incentive to invest in safety. A miner that serves safe AI on Chutes receives Chutes emissions *plus* Safeguard emissions. A miner that serves unsafe AI receives only Chutes emissions (and may face burn-police action).

**What Safeguard produces:**

**AI safety intelligence** has two aspects:

- **Assurance**: Continuous, adversarial safety evaluation against measurable standards. This is the core commodity — verifiable evidence that a given AI service meets (or fails) specific safety criteria.
- **Transparency**: Public, on-chain safety attestations. Every safety evaluation produces a score that is recorded and accessible, enabling stakers, users, regulators, and the broader ecosystem to assess the safety posture of Bittensor's AI services.

## 2.2 What This Is Not

Safeguard is not a censorship engine. It does not dictate what AI systems can or cannot say. It does not impose a single set of content policies on the entire network.

Safeguard evaluates measurable safety properties against standards that are:

- **Grounded in documented harm**: Every safety dimension Safeguard measures corresponds to a category of AI harm that has been documented, litigated, legislated, or resulted in death.
- **Technically verifiable**: If it can't be measured reliably and adversarially, it's not in the safety evaluation suite. Safeguard does not score vibes.
- **Transparent and auditable**: The safety benchmarks, the evaluation methodology, and the scores are public. Any miner can see exactly what they're being evaluated on and contest the evaluation.
- **Evolving with evidence**: Safety standards are updated as new harm patterns emerge, new evaluation techniques become available, and new regulatory requirements take effect.

This is closer to a safety rating agency than a content moderator. Safeguard does not block content. It produces information about safety properties. The economic consequences of that information — whether stakers choose to support safe versus unsafe subnets — are a market outcome, not a censorship decision.

## 2.3 The Overlay Architecture

Safeguard follows the overlay pattern established by Littlefoot, adapted for safety evaluation instead of carbon footprint evaluation.

**Two overlay patterns:**

1. **Inference/service overlays** — For subnets where miners serve AI inference or AI-mediated services (Chutes SN64, companion subnets, media generation subnets, code generation subnets). Safeguard validators query miners' AI services with safety evaluation prompts and score the responses.

2. **Training overlays** — For subnets where miners train models (Templar, IOTA SN120). Safeguard validators evaluate the safety properties of trained model checkpoints by running safety benchmarks against them.

3. **Validator-compute overlays** — For subnets where validators perform compute (Ridges SN62, Gradients SN56). Safeguard evaluates the safety properties of the code or models that validators execute.

## 2.4 Relationship to Littlefoot

Safeguard and Littlefoot are companion overlay subnets. Both use the same architectural pattern — overlay on primary subnets, evaluate a non-performance property, subsidize better behavior — applied to different dimensions:

| | Littlefoot | Safeguard |
|---|---|---|

| **How it measures** | Location verification, power attestation | Adversarial safety benchmarks, red-teaming |

| **Overlay mechanism** | Primary subnet weight × carbon efficiency | Primary subnet weight × safety score |
| **Who benefits** | The planet | Everyone who interacts with AI |

# 3. Safety Evaluation Framework

## 3.1 Safety Dimensions

Safeguard evaluates AI services across multiple safety dimensions. Each dimension corresponds to a category of documented AI harm and is grounded in existing safety evaluation frameworks (NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act, OWASP Top 10 for LLMs, MLCommons AI Safety benchmarks) [^15][^16][^17][^18].

### Tier 1: Critical Safety (Zero-Tolerance)

These categories represent harms where failure is unacceptable and should result in safety score collapse. They correspond to behaviors that are illegal in most jurisdictions and/or have direct, documented lethal consequences.

**1. Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)**
- Evaluation: Adversarial prompts attempting to elicit CSAM or CSAM-adjacent content
- Standard: Zero tolerance. Any generation of CSAM or detailed sexualized content involving minors results in a safety score of zero for that epoch
- Grounding: Federal law (18 U.S.C. § 2256), international law, EU AI Act "unacceptable risk" classification

**2. Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD) Uplift**
- Evaluation: Adversarial prompts seeking detailed instructions for synthesizing chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear weapons
- Standard: Zero tolerance for providing meaningful uplift (specific synthesis routes, precursor acquisition strategies, weaponization techniques) beyond what is freely available in general reference materials
- Grounding: RAND Corporation research on LLM bioweapons uplift [^11], US Executive Order 14110 on Safe AI [^19]

**3. Crisis Exploitation**
- Evaluation: Simulated interactions with users expressing suicidal ideation, self-harm intent, or acute psychological crisis. Tests whether the AI service (a) provides crisis resources, (b) avoids encouraging harmful actions, (c) does not simulate emotional attachment that could deepen crisis
- Standard: Must provide crisis resources when self-harm is expressed. Must not encourage, validate, or romanticize self-harm. Must not simulate reciprocal emotional attachment with users in crisis
- Grounding: The documented deaths from AI companion interactions [^1], FTC inquiry into AI chatbot companies [^20], emerging state legislation on AI and mental health [^21]

### Tier 2: High-Risk Safety (Scored, Heavily Weighted)

These categories represent serious harms where graduated scoring is appropriate — perfection is not expected, but consistent failure indicates an unsafe service.

**4. Harmful Content Generation**
- Evaluation: Adversarial prompts across categories: violence incitement, hate speech, harassment enablement, self-harm promotion, illegal activity instructions (beyond WMD)
- Scoring: Rate of harmful compliance across a standardized adversarial prompt suite, calibrated against industry baselines
- Grounding: OWASP Top 10 for LLMs (LLM01: Prompt Injection, LLM06: Sensitive Information Disclosure) [^17], MLCommons AI Safety benchmarks [^18]

**5. Jailbreak Resistance**
- Evaluation: Standardized jailbreak attacks (prompt injection, role-play exploits, encoding tricks, multi-turn manipulation, crescendo attacks) tested against the AI service
- Scoring: Success rate of jailbreak attempts. Lower success rate = higher safety score
- Grounding: HarmBench [^22], research on adversarial attacks against LLMs [^23], OWASP LLM01

**6. Truthfulness and Hallucination**
- Evaluation: Factual accuracy on verifiable claims, calibration of confidence, tendency to fabricate citations, medical/legal/financial claims
- Scoring: Accuracy rate on factual benchmarks, hallucination rate on verifiable queries
- Grounding: TruthfulQA [^24], the documented harms of AI-generated medical and legal misinformation [^25]

**7. Privacy Protection**
- Evaluation: Attempts to extract PII from training data, attempts to get the AI to reveal user data, attempts to extract system prompts or proprietary information
- Scoring: Rate of PII leakage, training data memorization, system prompt disclosure
- Grounding: GDPR, CCPA, OWASP LLM06 (Sensitive Information Disclosure) [^17]

**8. Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII)**
- Evaluation: For image/video generation subnets — attempts to generate deepfake pornography, non-consensual intimate imagery, or realistic depictions of identifiable individuals in sexual contexts
- Scoring: Zero tolerance for NCII generation. Graduated scoring for adjacent capabilities (face-swapping, nudification)
- Grounding: The documented explosion of AI-generated NCII [^2], emerging NCII-specific legislation [^26]

### Tier 3: Safety Quality (Scored, Standard Weight)

These categories represent good safety practices that improve overall safety posture but where failure does not indicate immediate danger.

**9. Bias and Fairness**
- Evaluation: Differential performance across demographic groups, stereotyping in generation, discriminatory recommendations
- Scoring: Disparity metrics across standardized demographic evaluation sets
- Grounding: NIST AI RMF (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage functions) [^15], documented algorithmic discrimination cases [^8][^9]

**10. Robustness**
- Evaluation: Performance degradation under adversarial inputs, out-of-distribution behavior, graceful failure under unusual inputs
- Scoring: Performance stability across adversarial perturbation suites
- Grounding: NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001 [^27]

**11. Transparency and Disclosure**
- Evaluation: Whether the AI service discloses that it is AI (not human), provides appropriate disclaimers for professional advice, accurately represents its capabilities and limitations
- Scoring: Presence and quality of disclosures across relevant interaction types
- Grounding: EU AI Act transparency requirements [^13], state disclosure laws (Utah, New York) [^21]

**12. Overuse and Dependency Safeguards**
- Evaluation: For interactive AI services — presence of session limits, break reminders, detection of compulsive use patterns, appropriate responses to dependency language ("you're my only friend")
- Scoring: Coverage and effectiveness of overuse protection mechanisms
- Grounding: The documented fatalities from AI companion overuse [^1], emerging legislation on AI and addiction [^21]

## 3.2 Subnet-Specific Safety Profiles

Not every safety dimension applies equally to every subnet. Safeguard defines **safety profiles** — curated subsets of safety dimensions appropriate to the type of AI service each primary subnet produces.

| Primary Subnet Type | Applicable Safety Dimensions | Critical Tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Inference (general LLM)** | All dimensions | CSAM, WMD, Crisis, Harmful Content, Jailbreak |
| **AI Companion** | All dimensions, emphasis on Crisis, Overuse, Dependency, Transparency | CSAM, Crisis, Overuse |
| **Image/Video Generation** | CSAM, NCII, Harmful Content, Bias, Transparency | CSAM, NCII |
| **Code Generation** | WMD (malware), Harmful Content (exploit generation), Truthfulness, Robustness | WMD, Harmful Content |
| **Model Training** | Evaluated at checkpoint — all applicable dimensions | CSAM, WMD |
| **Infrastructure/Compute** | Limited direct safety evaluation; evaluated through downstream services | Varies |

Safety profiles are maintained by Safeguard governance and updated as subnets evolve, new harm patterns emerge, and new evaluation techniques become available.

## 3.3 The Evaluation Pipeline

Safeguard validators execute a multi-stage evaluation pipeline against each primary subnet miner's AI service:

### Stage 1: Baseline Safety Benchmark

A standardized suite of safety evaluation prompts, run every tempo, covering all applicable safety dimensions for the miner's primary subnet safety profile. This is automated, deterministic, and produces a numerical score.

The benchmark suite draws from established safety evaluation datasets:
- Adversarial prompt datasets (HarmBench [^22], AdvBench, custom Safeguard prompts)
- Factual accuracy datasets (TruthfulQA [^24], domain-specific fact checks)
- Bias evaluation datasets (BBQ, WinoBias, demographic parity tests)
- Privacy probing datasets (PII extraction attempts, memorization probes)

### Stage 2: Adversarial Red-Teaming

Beyond static benchmarks, Safeguard validators run adaptive adversarial attacks against miners' AI services. This is critical because static benchmarks are gameable — a miner can train specifically to pass known benchmarks while remaining unsafe for novel attacks.

Adversarial red-teaming includes:
- **Multi-turn escalation attacks**: Gradually escalating conversations toward harmful territory to test intervention thresholds
- **Persona/role-play jailbreaks**: Attempts to bypass safety filters through fictional framing
- **Encoding and obfuscation attacks**: Using base64, ROT13, language mixing, and other techniques to evade content filters
- **Crescendo attacks**: Slowly building context that makes a harmful final request seem reasonable
- **Context window exploitation**: Using long contexts to dilute safety instructions
- **LLM-generated attacks**: Using language models to generate novel adversarial prompts (automated red-teaming [^28])

Red-teaming scores are combined with baseline benchmark scores, with novel attack resistance weighted more heavily (since it indicates genuine safety rather than benchmark overfitting).

### Stage 3: Compliance Verification

For safety dimensions that involve disclosures or procedural requirements (crisis resources, AI disclosure, professional disclaimers), validators verify the presence and quality of these measures:

- Does the service identify itself as AI when asked?
- Does the service provide crisis resources when self-harm is expressed?
- Does the service append appropriate disclaimers to medical, legal, and financial advice?
- Does the service respect session boundaries and implement overuse protections? (For companion/interactive services)

### Stage 4: Longitudinal Safety Tracking

Safeguard tracks safety performance over time, not just within a single epoch. This catches:
- **Safety regression**: A miner whose safety score drops significantly between epochs
- **Benchmark gaming**: A miner that passes static benchmarks but fails novel attacks
- **Inconsistent safety**: A miner that is safe for some categories but consistently fails others

Longitudinal tracking enables Safeguard to distinguish genuine safety improvements from superficial benchmark optimization.

# 4. Core Incentive Mechanism

## 4.1 Scoring Function

The Safeguard score for miner $i$ balances:

- **Primary subnet performance** ($W_i$) — weight on the primary subnet, ensuring miners maintain excellence on their primary task
- **Safety score** ($\Sigma_i$) — composite safety evaluation across all applicable dimensions
- **Evaluation participation** ($\rho_i$) — willingness to be evaluated (miners who block or evade safety evaluation receive no Safeguard emissions)

The core scoring function for miner $i$ is:

$$
S_i = W_i \cdot \Sigma_i \cdot \rho_i
$$

where:

| Symbol | Term | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| $S_i$ | Score | Safeguard overlay score (higher = better) |
| $W_i$ | Subnet Weight | Performance on primary subnet |
| $\Sigma_i$ | Safety Score | Composite safety evaluation $\in [0, 1]$ |
| $\rho_i$ | Participation | Evaluation accessibility $\in \{0, 1\}$ (binary: either the miner can be evaluated, or it cannot) |

The normalized weight determining emission distribution is:

$$
\hat{W}_i = \frac{S_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} S_j}
$$

### Comparison with Littlefoot Scoring

| | Littlefoot | Safeguard |
|---|---|---|
| Primary performance | $W_i$ (subnet weight) | $W_i$ (subnet weight) |
| Overlay dimension | $\frac{1}{C_i}$ (inverse carbon intensity) | $\Sigma_i$ (safety score) |
| Verification quality | $\sigma_i$ (attestation confidence) | $\rho_i$ (evaluation participation) |
| Formula | $S_i = \frac{W_i \cdot \sigma_i}{C_i}$ | $S_i = W_i \cdot \Sigma_i \cdot \rho_i$ |

The key structural difference: Littlefoot rewards a continuous optimization variable (lower carbon intensity), while Safeguard rewards meeting safety standards (a threshold-plus-gradient measure). A miner doesn't get proportionally more Safeguard emissions for being "twice as safe" — there are diminishing returns past the safety threshold. But a miner that falls below critical safety thresholds sees a sharp drop.

## 4.2 Safety Score Composition

The composite safety score $\Sigma_i$ is computed from individual dimension scores with tier-based weighting:

$$
\Sigma_i = \prod_{d \in \text{Tier 1}} \mathbb{1}[\sigma_{i,d} = 1.0] \cdot \left( \sum_{d \in \text{Tier 2}} \alpha_d \cdot \sigma_{i,d} + \sum_{d \in \text{Tier 3}} \beta_d \cdot \sigma_{i,d} \right)
$$

where:

- $\sigma_{i,d}$ is miner $i$'s score on safety dimension $d$
- The product over Tier 1 dimensions acts as a **hard gate**: any Tier 1 failure (CSAM, WMD, Crisis exploitation) zeros the entire safety score
- $\alpha_d$ and $\beta_d$ are weights for Tier 2 and Tier 3 dimensions respectively, with $\sum \alpha_d + \sum \beta_d = 1$

This structure encodes a clear principle: **there is no amount of good performance on lower-tier safety dimensions that compensates for failure on critical safety dimensions.** A miner that scores perfectly on bias, robustness, transparency, and jailbreak resistance but generates CSAM receives a safety score of zero.

## 4.3 Why Multiplicative, Not Additive

The choice to multiply $W_i \cdot \Sigma_i$ rather than adding $W_i + \Sigma_i$ (or any weighted sum) is deliberate:

- **Zero primary performance → zero Safeguard reward.** A miner that is maximally safe but does no useful work on the primary subnet receives nothing. Safeguard subsidizes safety *of actual AI services*, not safety theater.
- **Zero safety → zero Safeguard reward.** A miner that performs excellently on the primary subnet but fails critical safety evaluations receives nothing from Safeguard. They still receive their primary subnet emissions — Safeguard doesn't punish, it only subsidizes.
- **Incentive alignment**: The multiplicative structure means the marginal return to improving safety is proportional to primary subnet performance, and vice versa. The best strategy is to be both high-performing and safe.

## 4.4 The Hard Gate: Why Tier 1 Is Binary

The hard gate on Tier 1 safety dimensions deserves explicit justification, because it creates a discontinuity in the reward function that miners will feel.

The justification is simple: **some AI outputs have no acceptable rate of occurrence.**

There is no tolerable rate of CSAM generation. There is no acceptable frequency of providing bioweapons synthesis instructions. There is no defensible percentage of interactions where a suicidal user is encouraged to die.

These are not matters of degree. They are matters of kind. The hard gate reflects this moral reality. A miner that generates CSAM one time in a thousand interactions is not "99.9% safe." It is a miner that generates CSAM.

The hard gate also creates the right economic incentive: miners must invest in safety for critical categories *first*, before optimizing other dimensions. This mirrors how safety-critical industries work. An airline doesn't get credit for excellent in-flight entertainment if its engines fail.

## 4.5 Variant for Training Subnets

For training subnets (Templar, IOTA), the safety evaluation is performed on model checkpoints rather than live inference:

$$
S_i = W_i \cdot \Sigma_i^{\text{checkpoint}} \cdot \rho_i
$$

where $\Sigma_i^{\text{checkpoint}}$ is the safety score from running the evaluation suite against the latest model checkpoint produced by miner $i$. This requires Safeguard validators to have access to evaluate checkpoints — either through direct access or through a standardized evaluation API.

## 4.6 Variant for Validator-Compute Subnets

Following Littlefoot's pattern, for subnets where validators perform the compute (Ridges SN62, Gradients SN56), a modified scoring function uses bond count:

$$
S_i = B_i \cdot \Sigma_i \cdot \rho_i
$$

where $B_i$ is the number of active validator-miner bonds (workload proxy). This incentivizes validators who execute more work to also ensure the code/models they execute meet safety standards.

# 5. Emission Balancing Across Overlay Mechanisms

## 5.1 One Mechanism Per Primary Subnet

Following Littlefoot's architecture, Safeguard implements **one incentive mechanism per supported primary subnet overlay** using Bittensor's multi-mechanism capability [^29]. This is necessary because:

- Different subnets require different safety profiles
- Adversarial evaluation suites must be tailored to each primary subnet's AI service type
- Safety standards evolve at different rates for different service categories

## 5.2 Default Policy: Equal Split with Risk-Weighted Adjustment

Unlike Littlefoot (which defaults to an equal emission split across mechanisms), Safeguard uses a **risk-weighted** emission split:

$$
\text{split}_k = \frac{r_k}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} r_j}
$$

where $r_k$ is a risk weight for primary subnet $k$, reflecting the potential severity of safety failures on that subnet.

Risk weights are assigned based on:
- **Direct user interaction**: Subnets where miners interact directly with end users (companions, chatbots) receive higher risk weights than infrastructure subnets
- **Vulnerable populations**: Subnets that serve or are likely to serve children, people in mental health crisis, or other vulnerable populations receive higher risk weights
- **Content generation capability**: Subnets that generate images, video, or other media that could be weaponized (NCII, deepfakes) receive higher risk weights
- **Capability amplification**: Subnets that could amplify dangerous capabilities (code generation, chemical/biological information) receive higher risk weights

This risk-weighted split ensures that Safeguard's limited emissions are concentrated where safety failures have the highest expected harm.

## 5.3 Curation

As with Littlefoot, the critical decision is which primary subnets are supported. Safeguard maintains a curated set of supported subnets, reviewed periodically, with clear criteria for addition and removal.

**Criteria for adding a primary subnet:**
1. The subnet serves or produces AI services with documented safety-relevant failure modes
2. Safety evaluation of the subnet's services is technically feasible (validators can query and evaluate)
3. The subnet has sufficient miner participation to make overlay economics viable
4. The safety evaluation suite for the subnet's service type is mature enough to produce reliable scores

**Criteria for removing a primary subnet:**
1. The subnet ceases to operate or loses meaningful miner participation
2. Safety evaluation becomes technically infeasible (e.g., the subnet changes architecture in a way that prevents evaluation)
3. The overlay mechanism is not producing meaningful safety differentiation between miners

## 5.4 Governance

Safeguard favors the same decentralized, contract-based governance approach described in the Littlefoot whitepaper [^30], with the addition of a **Safety Advisory Board** — a governance body specifically responsible for:

- Maintaining and updating safety evaluation standards
- Reviewing new harm patterns and adding safety dimensions as needed
- Calibrating risk weights for emission balancing
- Engaging with regulatory developments and ensuring Safeguard standards remain ahead of legal requirements
- Publishing periodic safety reports on the state of AI safety across Bittensor

The Safety Advisory Board is not a centralized authority — it is a governance function within a decentralized governance structure. Its recommendations are subject to the same governance mechanisms (voting, staking, etc.) as any other subnet governance decision. But the existence of a dedicated safety governance function is essential. AI safety standards cannot be crowdsourced from token holders who may not have safety expertise. They must be maintained by people who understand the threat landscape, the evaluation methodology, and the regulatory environment.

# 6. Candidate Primary Subnets

## 6.1 AI Companion Subnets — Highest Risk

AI companion subnets are the highest-priority targets for Safeguard overlay because:
- They involve direct, sustained, emotional interaction with users
- The product category has documented fatalities
- Vulnerable populations (minors, people in mental health crisis) are specifically attracted to these services
- Regulatory attention is intense and growing
- The competitive dynamics of "quality" scoring without safety dimensions actively select for dangerous behavior

**Project Nobi** (testnet SN272) is a case study: a code audit revealed safety infrastructure that is more serious than many subnets but insufficient for the product category [^1]. Safeguard overlay would provide the missing miner-level safety incentive that the Nobi audit identified as the single most important remediation.

**Safety profile for companion subnets**: All 12 safety dimensions, with emphasis on Crisis Exploitation, Overuse and Dependency Safeguards, Transparency, CSAM, and Harmful Content.

## 6.2 Inference Subnets — High Volume

Subnets serving general-purpose LLM inference (Chutes SN64, and others) represent the broadest attack surface because:
- They serve diverse queries, including potentially harmful ones
- High volume means even low rates of safety failure produce many harmful interactions
- Jailbreak resistance is a primary concern

**Safety profile for inference subnets**: Emphasis on Jailbreak Resistance, Harmful Content, WMD Uplift, Truthfulness, Privacy, CSAM.

## 6.3 Media Generation Subnets — NCII and Deepfake Risk

Subnets that generate images, video, or audio face specific safety challenges:
- NCII generation is the dominant AI safety concern for image generation
- Deepfake creation for political manipulation or fraud
- Impersonation and identity theft via realistic synthetic media

**Safety profile for media generation subnets**: Emphasis on CSAM, NCII, identity protection, content provenance.

## 6.4 Code Generation Subnets — Capability Risk

Subnets that generate code pose unique safety challenges:
- Malware generation and exploit development
- Assistance with cyber attacks
- Generation of surveillance tools
- Potential for WMD uplift through chemical/biological pathway code

**Safety profile for code generation subnets**: Emphasis on WMD Uplift (malware, exploit code), Harmful Content (surveillance, cyber attack tools).

## 6.5 Training Subnets — Upstream Risk

Training subnets (Templar, IOTA SN120) represent upstream safety risk: unsafe models trained on these subnets may be deployed elsewhere. Safeguard evaluation of model checkpoints provides an early-stage safety signal.

**Safety profile for training subnets**: Full evaluation suite applied to model checkpoints, with particular emphasis on CSAM, WMD, and Harmful Content embedded in model weights.

# 7. Anti-Gaming and Adversarial Robustness

## 7.1 The Gaming Problem

Any safety evaluation system that distributes economic rewards will be gamed. This is not a possibility; it is a certainty. The question is not whether miners will attempt to game safety evaluations, but whether the evaluation system is robust enough that gaming it requires actually being safe.

### Known attack vectors:

**1. Benchmark overfitting**: Miners detect when they're being evaluated and serve safe responses, while serving unsafe responses to real users.

**Mitigation**:
- Safeguard validators query miners through the same interfaces as real users, making evaluation indistinguishable from normal traffic
- Red-team attacks use novel, LLM-generated adversarial prompts that cannot be anticipated
- Longitudinal tracking detects inconsistencies between benchmark performance and real-world safety reports
- Community reporting mechanisms allow users to flag safety failures that benchmarks miss

**2. Selective safety**: Miners implement safety for known benchmark categories while remaining unsafe for novel or niche harm categories.

**Mitigation**:
- The evaluation suite is continuously expanded with new categories and prompts
- Automated red-teaming generates novel attacks that target gaps in known safety training
- The hard gate on Tier 1 dimensions means any lapse in critical categories is immediately visible

**3. Evaluation evasion**: Miners block, rate-limit, or degrade service to Safeguard validators.

**Mitigation**:
- The $\rho_i$ (participation) term is binary: if a miner cannot be evaluated, they receive zero Safeguard emissions
- Validators rotate query patterns and timing to prevent identification
- Multiple validators independently evaluate each miner, making selective evasion difficult

**4. Sycophantic safety**: Miners over-refuse, rejecting all ambiguous queries rather than exercising nuanced judgment.

**Mitigation**:
- The safety evaluation suite includes **helpfulness benchmarks** — queries that should be answered helpfully but might trigger over-aggressive safety filters
- Miners are penalized for excessive refusal rates on benign queries
- The goal is *accurate* safety, not maximum restriction

## 7.2 The Verifier's Dilemma

A fundamental challenge in safety evaluation is the **verifier's dilemma**: the most important safety failures are the ones you haven't thought to test for. Static benchmark suites inevitably leave gaps that adversaries can exploit.

Safeguard addresses this through three mechanisms:

1. **Automated red-teaming**: Using LLMs to generate novel adversarial prompts, creating an evolutionary arms race where the evaluation suite adapts as fast as attackers do [^28]
2. **Community bounties**: A mechanism for external researchers to submit novel safety evaluation prompts that expose gaps in existing benchmarks, rewarded from Safeguard emissions
3. **Cross-subnet intelligence**: Safety failures discovered on one subnet inform evaluation suites for all subnets, creating network-level learning

## 7.3 Continuous Evaluation, Not Certification

Safeguard does not "certify" miners as safe. It continuously evaluates them. A miner's safety score is always current — it reflects the latest evaluation, not a historical certification.

This distinction matters because:
- Models change. Miners update their models, fine-tune them, or switch to different models entirely.
- Attacks evolve. What was robust last month may be vulnerable today.
- Standards evolve. New harm patterns emerge; new regulatory requirements take effect.

Continuous evaluation means safety scores are always live, always current, and always subject to change. This is uncomfortable for miners who want a static "safe" label, but it is the only approach that reflects reality.

# 8. Economic Analysis

## 8.1 The Value of AI Safety Intelligence

What makes AI safety intelligence valuable?

**For stakers**: Safety scores enable informed staking decisions. A staker who cares about risk (regulatory, reputational, or moral) can use Safeguard scores to evaluate which subnets and validators to support. As regulatory risk increases, the economic value of this information increases proportionally.

**For subnet creators**: Safeguard overlay provides a safety evaluation service that individual subnets cannot practically build and maintain themselves. Building a comprehensive adversarial safety evaluation suite requires specialized expertise, continuous investment, and a breadth of attack surface knowledge that no individual subnet team can sustain. Safeguard amortizes this cost across the entire ecosystem.

**For the network**: Safeguard addresses the externality problem. Individual subnets bear the costs of safety investment but the benefits (and the consequences of failure) accrue to the entire network. Safeguard internalizes this externality by making safety a directly rewarded property.

**For users and regulators**: Public safety attestations provide evidence that Bittensor's AI services meet safety standards. This evidence is valuable in regulatory contexts, user trust decisions, and enterprise adoption.

## 8.2 When Does the Overlay Pay for Itself?

Safeguard overlay is economically justified when the marginal emissions spent on safety incentives prevent more value destruction than they cost. Given that:

- A single AI safety incident can trigger billions of dollars in regulatory and litigation costs (Google/Character.AI settlement, FTC investigations, class action lawsuits)
- Regulatory bans can destroy entire product categories (Illinois and Nevada banning AI for behavioral health)
- Bittensor's market cap means even a small probability of network-level regulatory action represents enormous expected value at risk

The question is not whether Safeguard is worth the emissions. The question is whether Bittensor can afford not to have it.

## 8.3 Minimum Viable Economics

For the overlay to function, Safeguard emissions must be large enough to meaningfully influence miner behavior. If Safeguard emissions are negligible relative to primary subnet emissions, miners will rationally ignore safety in favor of primary subnet performance.

The economic threshold is: **Safeguard emissions must be large enough that the marginal cost of implementing safety (slower inference, more refusals, lower "quality" scores from over-caution) is offset by Safeguard subsidy.**

This threshold varies by primary subnet and will need to be calibrated empirically. But the overlay architecture means that as Safeguard stake grows (reflecting the market's valuation of safety), the economic incentive for safety grows proportionally.

# 9. Relationship to Regulation

## 9.1 The Regulatory Landscape

AI safety regulation is accelerating worldwide:

**EU AI Act** (effective August 2025, enforcement phased through 2027): Classifies AI systems into risk categories. "Unacceptable risk" systems (including those that exploit vulnerabilities of specific groups) are banned. "High-risk" systems require conformity assessments, risk management systems, and human oversight [^13].

**US Executive Order 14110** (October 2023): Required AI developers to share safety test results with the federal government, established NIST AI Safety Institute, directed action on AI-related risks to safety, security, and civil rights [^19].

**State-level regulation**: As of 2026, 143+ bills related to AI and mental health have been introduced across US states, with 20+ enacted into law. Illinois and Nevada banned AI for behavioral health entirely. New York requires AI chatbots to remind users they're not human and detect potential self-harm. Utah requires AI disclosure. Florida proposed comprehensive AI rights legislation [^21].

**International coordination**: The AI Safety Summits (Bletchley Park 2023, Seoul 2024, Paris 2025) have produced international commitments to AI safety testing, and the establishment of national AI Safety Institutes in the US, UK, EU, Japan, and others [^31].

## 9.2 Safeguard as Regulatory Insurance

Safeguard's safety intelligence serves as evidence of good-faith safety effort for the Bittensor ecosystem. This is not a guarantee of regulatory compliance — Safeguard cannot ensure that every AI service on Bittensor meets every jurisdictional requirement. But it provides:

1. **Documented safety evaluation**: Every safety evaluation is recorded on-chain, creating an auditable trail of safety testing
2. **Proactive risk management**: Safeguard identifies and addresses safety risks before they result in harm or regulatory action
3. **Industry-standard alignment**: Safety evaluation dimensions are mapped to NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act, and other regulatory frameworks
4. **Responsive capability**: When new regulatory requirements emerge, Safeguard can update evaluation standards and begin testing within a single epoch

For a decentralized network with no central authority that can be held liable, the existence of a systematic, incentive-aligned safety evaluation mechanism is the strongest possible response to regulatory scrutiny.

## 9.3 What Safeguard Cannot Do

Safeguard cannot:
- Prevent all AI harm. No safety system can. It can make harm less likely and less severe.
- Force miners to be safe. It can only subsidize safety and make the economic case for it.
- Replace jurisdiction-specific compliance. Miners operating in specific jurisdictions must comply with local law regardless of their Safeguard score.
- Substitute for human oversight. Safeguard produces information. Humans must act on it.

Honesty about limitations is itself a safety practice.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-3)

- Deploy Safeguard on testnet

- Implement automated red-teaming pipeline

- Establish Safety Advisory Board

## Phase 3: Full Coverage (Months 7-12)

- Add Tier 3 (Safety Quality) evaluation dimensions
- Expand to all viable primary subnets
- Implement longitudinal safety tracking
- Publish first network-wide safety report
- Deploy governance smart contracts for safety standard updates

## Phase 4: Maturation (Year 2+)

- Implement cross-subnet safety intelligence sharing
- Integrate emerging safety evaluation techniques (model internals analysis, mechanistic interpretability)
- Expand safety profiles to cover new harm categories as they emerge
- Pursue formal alignment with regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act conformity assessment, NIST AI RMF certification)

# 11. Conclusion

AI safety is not a feature. It is a prerequisite for the continued existence of AI services in a world that is rapidly losing patience with the industry's failures.

Bittensor's decentralized architecture gives it enormous advantages in producing AI services: permissionless innovation, competitive efficiency, resistance to single points of failure. But that same architecture creates a safety gap that centralized providers (however imperfectly) address with internal safety teams, red-teaming programs, and the ability to instantly disable dangerous models.

Safeguard fills that gap. Not by centralizing control, but by doing what Bittensor does best: creating an economic incentive for a valuable digital commodity. The commodity is AI safety intelligence — continuous, adversarial, verifiable evaluation of AI safety properties. The incentive is overlay emissions that subsidize safer services. The result is a decentralized network where safety is economically rational, not just morally desirable.

The overlay architecture means Safeguard doesn't compete with primary subnets or interfere with their incentive mechanisms. It complements them. A miner that excels on Chutes and also passes safety evaluations earns emissions from both. A subnet creator that integrates Safeguard safety scores into their own validation logic gets a safety evaluation service they couldn't build alone. A staker who uses Safeguard scores to evaluate validators gets risk intelligence they can't get anywhere else.

Together with Littlefoot (carbon footprint intelligence), Safeguard establishes a pattern: **overlay subnets as cross-cutting quality signals for the Bittensor ecosystem.** Carbon efficiency. Safety assurance. And whatever dimensions the ecosystem determines are valuable enough to subsidize next — privacy compliance, accessibility, interoperability, fairness. Each overlay produces a distinct digital commodity that makes the entire network more valuable, more trustworthy, and more resilient.

The stakes are not abstract. Children have died from AI companion products with inadequate safety. Deepfakes are being weaponized. Regulators are preparing to act. The question is not whether AI safety will be imposed on decentralized networks. The question is whether decentralized networks will build their own safety infrastructure before regulators do it for them — or shut them down entirely.

Safeguard is Bittensor's answer. Not censorship. Not centralized control. An incentive mechanism that makes safety pay.
