## ORIGIN AND CORE MOTIVATION

The Littlefoot subnet concept emerged during work on a previous "audit white paper" when the team discovered a fundamental transparency gap: nobody has any way to obtain information about energy production or usage in Bittensor. This lack of visibility into the network's environmental impact became the catalyst for designing a subnet specifically to address carbon footprint reduction.

## THE OVERLAY SUBNET ARCHITECTURE

### Core Design Philosophy
Littlefoot is designed as an "overlay subnet" - not the first of its kind (SN118 is mentioned as another overlay example that incentivizes long-term holding). The key characteristics:

1. **Dual Registration**: Miners must be registered and receiving emissions on a "primary subnet" (Lium, Chutes, Templar, etc.) while also registering on Littlefoot
2. **Additional Incentives**: Littlefoot provides supplementary rewards on top of primary subnet emissions, functioning as a "carbon subsidy" that covers the cost of cleaner infrastructure decisions
3. **Attestation-Based**: Miners provide attestations about their carbon usage and location, which validators verify
4. **Non-Disruptive Goal**: The mechanism aims to nudge existing miners toward more efficient decisions when making infrastructure choices, acting as a tie-breaker rather than a complete overhaul

### The Scoring Function
The basic formula discussed: **Weight on primary subnet × Signal strength ÷ Carbon rating**

Where:
- **Weight on primary subnet**: The miner's emission weight on their primary subnet (Lium, Templar, etc.)
- **Signal strength**: Quality/completeness of data reporting and attestations provided by the miner
- **Carbon rating**: A measure of carbon usage (lower is better), primarily based on geographic location

## CRITICAL DESIGN CHALLENGES DISCUSSED

### 1. Cross-Subnet Balancing Problem

**The Challenge**: Different subnets have vastly different carbon footprints. Git tensor miners likely use minimal compute compared to GPU-intensive subnets like Chutes or training subnets.

**The Dilemma**: How to fairly compare and balance rewards across subnets?
- Option A: Weight by subnet emissions (influences Bittensor network more directly)
- Option B: Weight by actual carbon impact (maximizes environmental benefit)

**Status**: Acknowledged as "probably the trickiest part of the subnet design" and "the part that I'm least sure about." The rebalancing function across mechanisms/subnets is the key unsolved piece.

### 2. Interference with Primary Subnet Incentive Mechanisms

**Core Concern**: Could Littlefoot disrupt the incentive mechanisms of primary subnets by changing miner rankings?

**Why It Matters**: If the subnet owner wants to incentivize certain behaviors, a large Littlefoot subnet could "fuck with the incentive mechanism" by changing the relative rewards miners receive.

**Mitigating Factors Identified**:
- The scoring function multiplies by original weighting, so it "shouldn't change rankings much except in really extreme circumstances"
- If Littlefoot is small relative to primary subnet, the interference is minimal
- If Littlefoot is large, it actually signals to subnet owners that the community cares about carbon efficiency, potentially motivating primary subnets to adjust
- For small subnets, Littlefoot could actually help by boosting emissions to their miners, serving as a bootstrapping mechanism

**Strategic Insight**: The relative size of Littlefoot versus primary subnets becomes a critical factor in whether it's seen as helpful or disruptive.

### 3. The Token Economics Problem (Death Spiral Risk)

**The Fundamental Problem**: Miners on overlay subnets may have no incentive to hold Littlefoot tokens because they don't care about it as much as their primary subnet.

**Comparison to SN118**: Similar overlay design where:
- Mechanism incentivizes buying baskets of tokens and holding
- Holders earn SN118 emissions
- Holders can sell those emissions and buy more basket tokens
- This creates a cycle that drives down SN118 price
- SN118 addressed this with delayed emissions or disincentives for selling

**The Littlefoot Variant**:
- Miners already doing clean work might receive Littlefoot emissions without changing behavior
- Since Littlefoot isn't their primary subnet, they can unstake everything immediately
- This creates massive net outflows from the subnet
- Large outflows mean lower effective emissions
- Could lead to a death spiral where the subnet becomes worthless

**Counterargument**: The issue affects all subnets - how to prevent immediate miner liquidity exits is a general problem. The difference with overlays is that they may be seen as less essential than "commodity subnets" that provide direct value.

### 4. The Git Tensor Problem (Gaming via Low-Quality Participation)

**Scenario A (Intended)**: Existing high-quality miners on primary subnets register on Littlefoot and adjust to cleaner infrastructure

**Scenario B (Gaming Risk)**: New miners:
1. See Littlefoot incentives
2. Register on Littlefoot first
3. Realize they need emissions on primary subnet (Templar, etc.)
4. Spam low-quality work on primary subnet just to get any emissions
5. Bloat primary subnet with garbage output
6. Collect Littlefoot rewards while providing minimal value to primary subnet

**Why This is Concerning**: Similar to Git tensor miners who "spam every repo that has some weights" to collect any emissions possible.

**Mitigating Factors**:
- The weight multiplication in the formula means low-performing primary miners get proportionally low Littlefoot rewards
- It's difficult to maintain competitive emissions on demanding primary subnets like Templar
- The design should attract already-competitive miners rather than making it easy for newcomers to game

**Remaining Risk**: If only the lowest-performing miners from each primary subnet join Littlefoot, the subnet wastes its emissions on miners who aren't doing most of the work on primary subnets anyway, failing to influence where the bulk of compute actually happens.

### 5. The "All Shitty Miners" Scenario

**The Problem**: What if a primary subnet's worst performers all register on Littlefoot?
- Within their Littlefoot cohort, some become "the best" relatively
- They receive significant Littlefoot emissions
- But they're not the miners actually doing the important work on the primary subnet

**Counter-mechanism**: 
- If decent miners on the primary subnet observe this, they should register on Littlefoot to capture those emissions
- The subnet's incentive mechanism should naturally attract better performers
- "You got to just trust the design, the subnet design and human consensus"

**Additional Safeguard Discussed**: Burn/recycle mechanism
- If all participating miners are "shitty" (low performing), don't distribute 100% of Alpha
- Reduce emissions proportionally to how poor the overall participant quality is
- As carbon footprint improves collectively, release more Alpha to participants
- Prevents "wasting money" or "flushing it down the toilet" when not making meaningful impact

### 6. UID Pressure Assumptions vs. Reality

**Original Assumption**: The design counted on UID pressure ensuring that only miners doing valuable work maintain their keys on primary subnets.

**Reality Check**: This assumption "is not realistic" because:
- On subnets like Lium, miners aren't incentivized to register many hotkeys
- They can stack all GPUs under one hotkey
- Therefore it's "very easy to stay registered on Lium"
- Low-performing miners can maintain registration with low weights rather than being booted off

**Design Implication**: 
- Can't rely on primary subnets automatically removing poor performers
- Instead, need to tune the scoring formula to handle the "long tail of low emission keys"
- The threshold/curve in the formula becomes critical
- Need to model different scenarios to determine proper weights

**Philosophical Tension**: 
- The design wants to reward work that's "good enough" on the primary subnet
- It assumes primary subnets ruthlessly boot miners doing inadequate work
- But reality shows many subnets allow low-weight miners to persist indefinitely
- This creates ambiguity about where to draw the line

### 7. The Threshold Tuning Challenge

**Scenario Explored**: Two miners on the same primary subnet
- Miner A: Extremely low carbon (Iceland), but low emissions on primary subnet
- Miner B: Top performer on primary subnet, but heavy carbon footprint (Germany/Poland)

**Design Question**: Who should rank higher?

**Design Intuition Articulated**:
- "As long as they're good enough to keep their spot, then by design, I want them to be getting that"
- If a miner is "dying on the primary subnet" or "not maintaining it stably," stop incentivizing them
- The formula needs tuning to find the right balance
- Don't want to incentivize the Iceland miner "just because he's in Iceland, if he just does shitty work"

**Refinement Through Discussion**:
- Initial thought: UID pressure solves this (proven unrealistic)
- Scaling by weight should handle low performers, just need to pick the right threshold/curve
- Need to work through scenarios with the formula to see what weights validators would actually assign
- Still requires "constant fine tuning" for each mechanism/primary subnet

**Underlying Principle**: 
- Want to reward miners good enough to stay on primary subnet
- Among those, incentivize the ones best at carbon usage
- Primary subnet determines quality threshold (Littlefoot shouldn't second-guess that)
- But can't actually rely on primary subnets enforcing quality thresholds aggressively

## SUBNET SCOPE CONSIDERATIONS

### Suitable Subnets (High Potential)
The design only works for subnets where:
1. **Miners perform compute work** (not just validators)
2. **Miners control their own infrastructure** (can choose locations/providers)
3. **Workloads are latency-tolerant** (can handle geographic distance to clean grids)
4. **High energy usage** (meaningful carbon impact)

**Specific Candidates Mentioned**:
- **Lium**: GPU rental via SSH (latency-tolerant, hardware verification already exists)
- **Chutes**: Serverless inference (uses Latitude.sh per their README, but latency concerns for real-time workloads)
- **Templar**: Training subnet (extremely high energy, latency-tolerant)
- **Tao Hash (SN14)**: Bitcoin mining subnet mentioned as future target, though higher integration effort

### Unsuitable Subnets
**Example: Gradients**
- Miners only submit fine-tuning code
- Validators run all the actual compute
- Miners themselves have no carbon footprint to optimize
- Littlefoot mechanism doesn't apply to this architecture

**Potential V2 Solution**: Support validator-focused subnets by targeting validators instead of miners, since validators often represent significant carbon footprint (up to 64 validators from 256 slots, each recomputing miner work).

### Special Case: Validators
**The Challenge**: 
- Validators do represent significant compute/carbon, especially when validation is expensive
- But reducing validator carbon footprint is harder because it requires validators to relocate
- Example given: "Round table can't move their bunker to some different country"
- Location-based carbon reduction works for mobile/flexible miners, less so for established validator infrastructure

**Possible Future Direction**: Once blockchain validators exist in Bittensor, they could be candidates for this type of mechanism.

## VERIFICATION AND DATA SOURCES

### Current Verification Approaches
**Low-Hanging Fruit**:
- Not many miners run on AWS/GCP in examined subnets (good for carbon, would make verification easier but less urgent)
- Many use bare metal services
- Can read operating location from bare metal provider APIs
- Location dominates carbon impact, making it the right starting point

**Provider API Approach**:
- Services like Latitude.sh provide location data via APIs
- Can verify where miners are actually operating
- Enables verification without self-reporting

### Signal Strength (σ in formula)
Represents quality/completeness of attestations:
- Has a miner provided all required data?
- Given necessary API access for verification?
- Partial credit system for incomplete reporting
- Could do "graded reporting" where miners get partial signal strength for partial compliance

## ECONOMIC ANALYSIS AND REAL-WORLD CONTEXT

### Location and Cost Correlation
**Key Insight from Discussion**: Clean grids often correlate with lower costs, so Littlefoot doesn't necessarily need to "overcome a green premium."

**Supporting Evidence Discussed**:
- Many clean locations have cheap electricity
- Cold climates (Iceland, Quebec, Norway) also reduce cooling costs
- Littlefoot can "tip close decisions" rather than forcing expensive changes

### Scale Requirements
**Consensus**: For meaningful influence, Littlefoot needs to be roughly 20-50% the size of target subnets.
- Too small: minimal influence, just token gesture
- Right-sized: tips infrastructure decisions, creates meaningful incentive
- Very large: could interfere with primary subnet mechanisms (see earlier concerns)

### The Compound Effect Vision
Even a small Littlefoot can trigger a virtuous cycle:
1. Some miners choose clean grids for bonus
2. Clean miners earn more (Littlefoot + cheaper power in many cases)
3. They reinvest and grow faster
4. Dirty-grid miners fall behind competitively
5. New miners observe and choose clean locations from the start
6. Clean datacenters see demand, expand capacity
7. Cycle repeats and accelerates

**Key Insight**: "Littlefoot doesn't need to overcome a green premium—it just needs to tip close decisions and let compound effects do the rest."

### Bitcoin Parallel Mentioned
Bitcoin mining has naturally moved toward efficiency out of economic necessity:
- Miners build their own solar farms to reduce power costs
- Economic pressure drove carbon reduction without explicit carbon incentives
- Bittensor could achieve similar outcomes through explicit incentive design

## DATA CENTER LOCATION POLITICS

**The Big Picture Issue**: 
Major corporations build data centers in environmentally problematic locations due to factors other than electricity cost:
- Example: Virginia and Ohio are among dirtiest AWS regions (coal states)
- But they're also the cheapest
- Bittensor can take "a principled stance against using the data centers that these companies are building in places that they shouldn't be for environmental reasons"

**Littlefoot's Role**: 
Can't directly influence where big corporations build data centers, but can:
- Influence where Bittensor miners choose to operate
- Create market signals about demand for clean compute
- Demonstrate that decentralized networks can embody environmental principles that centralized providers ignore

## LAUNCH STRATEGY AND NEXT STEPS

### Technical Implementation
**Key Requirement**: Need to develop specific validator code for each primary subnet mechanism
- Different subnets provide different data
- Each needs customized verification approach
- Start with "low-hanging fruit" candidates where verification is straightforward

### Go-to-Market Considerations
**Bitstarter Mentioned**: 
- Organization that helps launch subnets
- Could be valuable partner for bringing Littlefoot to market
- Especially useful if subnet is crowdfunded

**Educational Value Emphasized**:
"The best way to learn how subnets work is through building one, and even if it fails, it's like, you still learn a lot through that."

### Starting with 3-4 Target Subnets
**Pragmatic Launch Approach**:
- Don't try to support all subnets at once
- Pick 3-4 strong candidates where mechanism clearly works
- Build specific verification for those
- Expand as subnet matures and learns what works

**Ideal Candidates for V1**:
- Training subnets (highest energy impact)
- Latency-tolerant inference/compute
- Subnets where miners use identifiable providers (Latitude.sh, etc.)
- High UID pressure or at least decent performance requirements

## PHILOSOPHICAL TENSIONS UNRESOLVED

### Trust vs. Verification
The design relies on primary subnets to enforce quality, but:
- Can't actually verify that primary subnets do this effectively
- "You got to just trust the design, the subnet design and human consensus"
- Yet the whole point of Littlefoot is to add verification for something (carbon) that isn't naturally verified

### Influence vs. Interference
Want to:
- Influence miner decisions toward cleaner compute
- NOT interfere with primary subnet incentive mechanisms
- Support primary subnets while remaining independent

This requires careful sizing and formula tuning, but the exact balance remains uncertain.

### Individual Optimization vs. Collective Impact
The formula rewards individual miners for being clean, but:
- If only marginal miners participate, it wastes emissions
- If high-performers participate, it could disrupt primary subnet rankings
- Need collective participation from competitive miners without disrupting their primary focus

## RECURRING THEMES

1. **Constant Fine-Tuning Required**: Nearly every design element requires ongoing adjustment and calibration
2. **Trust in Emergence**: Multiple points where discussion concludes "trust the incentive mechanism to work itself out"
3. **Verification is Hard**: Easier to verify location than actual carbon usage, but location is also the dominant factor
4. **Size Matters**: Littlefoot's effectiveness and safety both scale with its size relative to primary subnets
5. **Learn by Doing**: Strong emphasis on launching and iterating rather than solving everything upfront

2. Is the core formula (W × σ / C) actually the right function?
3. What threshold separates "good enough" from "too poor to incentivize"?
4. How to prevent death spiral from miner token outflows?
5. Should validators be targeted in future versions?
6. How to handle subnets without UID pressure?
7. What burn/recycle mechanism parameters would work best?

## KEY SUCCESS METRICS IMPLIED

1. **Participation from high-performers**: Need top miners on primary subnets to register, not just marginal players
2. **Actual migration**: Miners should relocate or choose cleaner infrastructure due to incentives
3. **Non-interference**: Primary subnet owners shouldn't complain or feel disrupted
4. **Token stability**: Avoid death spiral from excessive miner outflows
5. **Measurable impact**: Real reduction in Bittensor's carbon footprint over time
6. **Scalability**: Mechanism should work across multiple primary subnets with different characteristics
